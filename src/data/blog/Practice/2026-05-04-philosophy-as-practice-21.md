## An overview of the past weeks

We started by talking about wisdom ([session 1](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice01/)). Then, our first reading was about practices of writing, and [practices beyond writing](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice02/), from Richard Shusterman.

After that, we talked about philosophy as the [care of the self](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice03) (Foucault), and as a *technology* of the self ([session 4](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice04)).

We moved on to explore ritual as practice in Confucius ([session 5](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice05)), and in Xunzi ([session 6](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice06)). And we also explored the strange, rambling philosophy of Zhuangzi, and the idea of free and easy wandering ([session 7](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice07)).

There's something pleasurable about just rambling about, so the session after ([session 8](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice08)), we talked about pleasure in Epicureanism. And, because there's a lot to say about pleasure, [in the session after that](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice09/).

For Epicurus, some pleasures are natural, and some are unnatural. So we enlisted the help of the Cynics ([session 11](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice11) and [session 12](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice12)) to talk about what is natural, and to explore some radical practices of freedom.

The next two sessions ([session 13](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice13) and [session 14](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice14)) were on Stoicism, which took up the challenge of asking "what even is nature"—and built a more systematic philosophy of practice, based on logic, ethics and physics.

Of all the philosophies we've explored together, two have become fashionable in the present: Stoicism and Buddhism. So in [session 15](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice15) and [session 16](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice16), we explored Buddhism and mindfulness. And we then looked at some variations on the Buddhist theme through exploring Zen / Chan ([session 17](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice17)) and Korean Sǒn ([session 18](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice18)).

Finally, we explored ways of queering philosophical practice through María Lugones's work in [session 19](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice19) and [session 20](https://courses.willbuckingham.com/posts/practice/practice20).

## Questions (looking back)

1. Which readings did you find most compelling or interesting?
2. Which did you like the least?
3. Which practices did you find most useful / interesting / challenging?
4. Of all these texts, which one most appealed to you **as writing**? Why?
5. What questions do you now have that you didn't have at the beginning of the course?
