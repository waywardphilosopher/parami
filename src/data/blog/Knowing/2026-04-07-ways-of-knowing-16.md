## The council of Pecans

In this section, Kimmerer tells the story of her grandfather gathering pecan nuts. The story also takes us into the late 19th and early 20th century history of North America, and the removal of indigenous peoples from their homelands. And it explores the mysterious phenomenon of "mast fruiting." We'll watch this [video about indigenous schools first](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-4wExQG9Dyk).

1. In what ways have traditional forms of knowledge been suppressed by more dominant forms of knowledge in your community?
2. Kimmerer writes, "All flourishing is mutual." What does she mean by this? And is she right?
3. What is mast fruiting? What does it say about the natural world?
4. What does Kimmerer learn from the pecans? And how? What does it mean to learn not just *about*, but also *from* the natural world?

If you are interested in mast fruiting, this [article on the history of the term]([https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstb.2020.0423](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstb.2020.0423)) is fascinating.
